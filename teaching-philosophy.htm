Teaching Philosophy

As future engineers, my students will be expected to effectively communicate their ideas, so it follows that I must model that same quality in order to be an effective teacher. Learning is never a one-sided endeavor; whether it be teacher-student interaction or self-study, acquisition of knowledge requires active study on behalf of the learner and clear, reasonable expectations for learning set forth by the educator (or material). During lectures, my seniors are often engaged in class-wide discussion and challenged to think critically about open-ended problems that may not have a single correct answer. We build on topics from previous courses, question simplifying assumptions, delve into a world of uncertainty, and in-so-doing transform our students' process of tackling real-world issues from passive calculation to synthesizing a solution strategy.

Teaching chemical engineering often involves highly abstract concepts or conversation about processes that students do not get to experience first-hand in the classroom, as our field involves very large, expensive equipment. Additionally, how fluids and material behave within these processes is not directly observable, so educators must relate lecture material to things found in everyday life. A topic like heat integration—which involves multiple networked heat transfer equipment to achieve an energy-efficient goal—can be explained in terms of your favorite Starbucks Frappuccino®. You wouldn't add a hot shot of espresso directly to crushed ice, because all the ice will melt and your product will lose its consistency; instead, you should first add the espresso to cold milk before pouring it over ice. By analogy, you wouldn't mix your hottest and coldest process streams first, because it leaves less energy available for heating or cooling other streams.

The role of an effective teacher is to both inspire young minds and provide guidance for achieving their goals. Effective communication is the key to doing those successfully. I was humbled last year when graduating seniors recognized me as the 2018 Outstanding Teaching Assistant of the Year for our department. They have emulated the critical thinking and communication skills modeled by myself and necessary to complete the senior capstone design graduation requirement, which will serve as their footing for entering the engineering profession.
